Air Pollution Grade Methodology

1. We collected data from the US Census Bureau, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), and County Health Rankings and Roadmaps.

2. From the EPA API, we chose to use the five most-tracked air pollutants, which are Carbon Monoxide, Ground-Level Ozone, Particulate Matter, Nitrogen Dioxide, and Sulfur Dioxide. However, the EPA did not have pollutant data for every single county in the United States.

3. We used a K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) Imputer algorithm to fill in missing air pollutant values. The KNN algorithm uses the proximity of one data point to others to accurately predict missing values. We used population, race, and income data of every county from the US Census Bureau API to make these predictions.

4. Then, we wanted to find what proportion each of these pollutants contributed to the deterioration of the physical environment. We used the physical environment rankings from the County Health Rankings and Roadmaps data as a basis for evaluation.

5. We ran a Random Forest Regression model (with optimized hyperparameters) to predict the physical environment rankings of counties from the revised air pollution data. Next, we extracted the feature importances of each pollutant in the Random Forest prediction to identify how much each pollutant impacted the physical environment.

6. We used these feature importances as "weights" to calculate a weighted average pollution score for each county. Then, we assigned numerical ranks to each county based on pollution scores.

7. For simplicity, we split all counties into quintiles, and assigned each quintile a grade of A, B, C, D, or F, with A representing counties with the least air pollution.

Map Note: In the US Census survey, people may choose to provide two or more races. This may lead to race percentages displayed on the map adding up to more than 100% for some counties.
